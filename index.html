चुड़ैल — ਚੁੜੇਲ
Churel
She died in childbirth, abandoned by those she loved. Now she wanders the crossroads at midnight — her feet reversed, her sari drenched in the blood of men who wronged her. If you hear a woman weeping at 3AM near a peepal tree, do not look back.

Her eyes are hollow pits filled with dead stars. Her touch turns the living to ash. She calls your name in a voice like your mother's — and that is how she catches you.

ब्रह्मराक्षस
Brahmaraakshas
A Brahmin scholar who abused his sacred knowledge — cursed to roam as a demon for eternity. He has four arms, three eyes, and a hunger for human brains that transcends death. He is the most feared entity in North Indian folklore.

He haunts ancient libraries, crumbling universities, and wells near temples. If you smell sandalwood and rot together at night — run. He is already behind you, chanting the Vedas in reverse.

ਆਤਮਾ — आत्मा
The Unrest Soul / Aatma
A Punjabi warrior who fell in battle before completing his oath. His aatma wanders the mustard fields and old havelis of Punjab, weeping blood. He wears the ghost of his turban, his kara glinting in moonlight that doesn't exist.

He stands at windows. He sits at the foot of your bed. He waits. He never speaks — but in the morning you will find blue handprints on your wall, and you will never be able to wash them off.

पिशाच — ਪਿਸ਼ਾਚ
Pishach — The Flesh Eater
Born from the sins of murderers and butchers, the Pishach feeds on corpses and the nearly dead. He is tall, black-skinned, his eyes burning like furnace coals, his mouth permanently stained red. He haunts cremation grounds, hospitals, and battlefields.

He possesses the living — making them speak in dead tongues, eat earth, and forget their own children's faces. If you feel suddenly cold in a hot room, and smell something rotting nearby — he has already entered you.

डायन — ਡਾਇਣ
Dayan — The Witch
She walks in the skins of old women, her true form hidden behind milky eyes and a curved back. She eats the lives of children and the dreams of young mothers. Her hair is always tangled with the bones of sparrows.

In Punjab, she is known to sit at the mouth of wells, whispering children's names. She can be in two places at once. She drinks moonlight and bleeds purple. The only warning is her laughter — which sounds exactly like a lullaby your grandmother used to sing.
